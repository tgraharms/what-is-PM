Part 2 / The document

What "Define" actually produces

The PRD is the deliverable that scares newcomers most, yet it is just a shared agreement on what you are building and why. Here is a short one for guest checkout. Flip the notes on to see what each part is doing.

PRD_guest-checkout.md Owner: You (PM)v0.3IN REVIEW
01  Problem

60% of shoppers abandon at the "create account" step. Interviews show most only wanted a one-time purchase and saw signup as a roadblock.

This comes straight from Discovery. A PRD opens with the real problem, never with the solution.
02  Goal & success metric

Lift checkout completion rate by 15% within 6 weeks of full launch. Guardrails: account signups fall no more than 5%, and fraud does not rise.

One clear number the whole feature is judged against. The guardrails name what you refuse to break while chasing it.
In scope
  • Buy without an account
  • Capture email for the receipt
  • Offer account creation after purchase
Out of scope
  • Saved payment methods
  • Order history for guests
  • Social login
Naming what you will not build is half the value of a PRD. It stops scope creep before it starts.
03  User stories
  • As a first-time shopper, I want to buy without making an account so I can finish quickly.
  • As a returning buyer, I still want to log in for my saved details.
Written from the user's point of view, not the system's. This keeps the team aimed at the person, not the code.
04  Open questions
  • What if the guest's email already belongs to an account?
  • How are refunds handled with no account to log into?
A good PRD admits what is still unknown. Open questions get resolved with design and engineering, not guessed alone.

The part that surprises people

It never actually ends.

Launch is not the finish line. You measure, you learn, the result raises a new question, and you are back at discovery. Good PMs get comfortable with that loop, because that is the job.
